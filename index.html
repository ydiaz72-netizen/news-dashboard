Global Overview

Today in the News

  • Iran's state media broadcast the first statement attributed to new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, who vowed to keep the Strait of Hormuz closed and continue attacks on US bases in the region; however, Iran's UN ambassador Amir Saeid Iravani separately told media that Tehran does not plan to block the strait, signaling internal contradictions in Iranian messaging.
  • Oil prices rose above $100 per barrel again as Iran stepped up attacks on energy infrastructure and shipping across the Gulf, with six ships struck in under 48 hours and targets hit in Bahrain, Oman, Dubai, and Kuwait; analysts described the disruption as the largest supply shock in the history of oil markets, and the US ordered a release from its Strategic Petroleum Reserve to calm markets.
  • Israel struck Beirut's southern suburbs and ordered a fresh evacuation of southern Lebanon after Hezbollah and Iran launched coordinated attacks on more than 50 targets including Israeli military bases; Lebanese authorities reported at least 12 killed along Beirut's seafront, and hundreds of thousands of civilians fled southward amid chaotic conditions.
  • The Israeli military dropped charges against five soldiers accused of violent abuse and sexual assault of a Palestinian detainee at the Sde Teiman detention facility, with the military's top lawyer citing "exceptional circumstances"; the decision drew international criticism and closed a yearlong legal case.
  • A French soldier was killed and others wounded in a drone attack in Iraq's Kurdish region, after which a pro-Iranian militia warned that French interests in Iraq and the region would be targeted following the deployment of a French aircraft carrier; a US refueling plane also crashed in Iraq in what Central Command said was not due to hostile or friendly fire.
  • The Pentagon told US lawmakers that war costs had exceeded $11.3 billion in the first six days of the conflict, with the true total unknown; separately, 46 mostly Democratic US senators called for an investigation into a strike on an Iranian school that reportedly killed at least 165 people, including children.
Forward Look

What to Expect Next

  • Historically, prolonged closure or disruption of the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of global oil passes — has triggered cascading economic effects; analysts expect continued elevated oil prices, fuel shortages in import-dependent nations such as Australia, and potential food price inflation in developing countries if the disruption persists beyond days.
  • The contradictory statements from Iran's new supreme leader and its UN ambassador suggest an internal power struggle or deliberate ambiguity; analysts expect diplomatic channels, possibly through intermediaries like India or Gulf Arab states, to test whether a negotiated de-escalation on the Strait of Hormuz is feasible in the coming days.
  • The US Senate Armed Services Committee is planning its first public oversight hearings on the Iran war; historically, such hearings during active conflicts have shaped public debate and occasionally constrained executive military action, and analysts expect scrutiny of the school strike and war costs to intensify domestic political pressure.
  • Israel's renewed strikes on Lebanon and its escalation against Hezbollah, combined with Iran's coordination with proxy forces across Iraq and the Gulf, suggest the conflict may widen further; historically, multi-front Middle East escalations have drawn in additional state and non-state actors, and military analysts expect continued proxy attacks on Western and Gulf targets.
  • Pakistan's airstrikes on Kabul and Afghan border provinces represent a significant escalation separate from the Iran conflict; analysts expect heightened tensions between Islamabad and the Taliban government, with potential refugee movements and further destabilization of the Afghanistan-Pakistan border region.

Middle East

Today in the News

  • Iran's state media broadcast what it described as the first statement by new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, declaring that the Strait of Hormuz would remain closed and that attacks on US bases in the region would continue. However, Iran's UN ambassador Amir Saeid Iravani separately told Euronews that Tehran does not plan to block or mine the strait, exposing an apparent contradiction in Iranian messaging.
  • Iranian forces escalated attacks on energy and transport infrastructure across the Gulf, striking targets in Bahrain, Oman, Kuwait, and Dubai, and hitting two foreign fuel tankers near Iraq, bringing the total vessels attacked during the conflict to 18. Brent crude remained above $100 per barrel, with analysts describing the disruption as the largest supply shock in oil market history.
  • Israel struck Beirut's southern suburbs and ordered a fresh evacuation of southern Lebanon after Hezbollah and Iran launched coordinated attacks on more than 50 targets including Israeli military bases. At least 12 people were reported killed along Beirut's seafront, and an Iranian missile struck a residential area in northern Israel.
  • Israel's military advocate general dropped charges against five reserve soldiers accused of sexually and physically abusing a Palestinian detainee at Sde Teiman detention facility, citing "exceptional circumstances." The case had drawn international attention after the detainee was hospitalized with broken ribs and a torn rectum.
  • Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu confirmed that Israeli strikes killed several Iranian nuclear scientists. Israeli security sources separately told The Guardian that the strikes on Iran were launched without a clear plan for regime change, suggesting that control of enriched uranium may become the ultimate measure of success.
  • A French soldier was killed and others wounded in a drone strike in Iraq's Kurdish region, with a pro-Iranian group claiming responsibility and warning that French interests in the region would be targeted following the deployment of a French aircraft carrier. The Pentagon disclosed that US war costs had exceeded $11.3 billion in the first six days of the conflict.
Middle East — Forward Look

What to Expect Next

  • Analysts expect the Strait of Hormuz disruption to deepen the global energy crisis in coming days; historically, even partial closures of the strait — through which roughly 20% of global oil passes — have triggered sustained price spikes and emergency reserve drawdowns by consuming nations.
  • The contradictory signals from Iran's new supreme leader and its UN ambassador suggest an internal power struggle or a negotiating tactic; historically, transitions of supreme leadership in Iran have involved periods of factional contestation, and analysts expect further mixed messaging as Mojtaba Khamenei seeks to consolidate authority.
  • With Hezbollah and Iran coordinating multi-front attacks on Israel while Israel escalates in Lebanon, analysts expect the conflict to widen further along the Israel-Lebanon border; historically, Israeli evacuation orders in southern Lebanon have preceded major ground or air operations.
  • The US Senate Armed Services Committee is planning the first public oversight hearings on the Iran war; historically, congressional scrutiny during active military operations has influenced the scope and duration of US engagements, and analysts expect bipartisan pressure on the administration over costs and strategy.
  • Iran's attacks on Gulf state infrastructure — including in the UAE, Bahrain, and Kuwait — are historically unprecedented in scale and may push Gulf Cooperation Council states toward more active military or diplomatic roles; analysts expect intensified backchannel diplomacy involving Gulf states, China, and India in the coming days.
