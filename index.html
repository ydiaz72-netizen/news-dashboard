Global Overview

Today in the News

  • A Pentagon preliminary investigation indicated that a U.S. Tomahawk cruise missile struck Shajareh Tayyebeh Elementary School in Minab, Iran, killing at least 165–175 people, mostly children, with officials citing outdated targeting data; a Republican senator called on the administration to publicly acknowledge responsibility if confirmed.
  • Iran escalated attacks on Gulf energy infrastructure, striking oil tankers in Iraqi waters, a fuel storage facility in Bahrain, and an oil facility at Oman's Port of Salalah, while the U.S. military said it destroyed 16 Iranian mine-laying vessels in the Strait of Hormuz amid fears of naval mine deployment in the critical shipping lane.
  • Oil prices surpassed $100 per barrel again as coordinated emergency petroleum reserve releases by multiple countries failed to calm global markets; the first six days of the war cost the U.S. an estimated $11.3 billion according to the Pentagon, and airlines including Air Transat and Qantas began raising fares due to surging jet fuel costs.
  • Iran's Cyprus ambassador confirmed that new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei was wounded in the strike that killed his father, former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, and six family members; Mojtaba Khamenei has not appeared publicly since being announced as leader three days prior.
  • Israel launched large-scale strikes on southern Beirut suburbs and central Beirut's waterfront, killing at least seven people, after Hezbollah and Iran carried out a joint attack on more than 50 Israeli military targets; the UN Security Council adopted a resolution — cosponsored by 135 countries — condemning Iran's attacks on Gulf states.
  • Iran's sports minister announced the country would withdraw from the 2026 FIFA World Cup, while Australia granted asylum to seven members of Iran's national women's soccer team, one of whom subsequently indicated she wished to return to Iran.
Forward Look

What to Expect Next

  • Analysts expect continued Iranian disruption of shipping in the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of global oil passes — and historically, mine-laying campaigns in the Gulf (as during the 1980s Iran-Iraq "Tanker War") have proven difficult to fully neutralize, suggesting prolonged energy market volatility.
  • The confirmed U.S. school strike is likely to intensify domestic and international political pressure on the Trump administration; historically, acknowledged civilian casualty incidents — such as the 2003 Baghdad market strikes — have eroded coalition support and complicated war objectives.
  • With tens of thousands of residents and tourists already departing Dubai and financial institutions evacuating Gulf offices, analysts expect further economic disruption across Gulf states, potentially accelerating diplomatic efforts for a ceasefire, particularly from Turkey and EU member states seeking to contain regional spillover.
  • Iran's warning that protesters will be treated as enemies, combined with U.S. calls for an Iranian uprising, raises the prospect of internal unrest; historically, as seen after the 1991 Gulf War in Iraq, externally encouraged uprisings without sustained support have produced volatile and unpredictable outcomes.
  • Hezbollah's escalation in Lebanon and the question of whether Yemen's Houthis will reopen Red Sea hostilities suggest the conflict may widen further; analysts note that historically, multi-front Middle East conflicts have proven far more difficult to de-escalate than single-theater engagements.

Middle East

Today in the News

  • A preliminary U.S. military assessment found that a U.S. Tomahawk cruise missile, reportedly guided by outdated targeting data, struck the Shajareh Tayyebeh Elementary School in Minab, Iran, killing at least 165–175 people, most of them children; a Republican senator called on the U.S. to publicly acknowledge responsibility if confirmed.
  • Iran escalated attacks on maritime and energy infrastructure across the Gulf, striking oil tankers in Iraqi waters with explosive-laden unmanned boats, setting ablaze a fuel storage facility in Bahrain, and hitting three merchant vessels near the Strait of Hormuz, prompting oil prices to surpass $100 per barrel again despite coordinated emergency reserve releases by consuming nations.
  • The U.S. military said it destroyed 16 Iranian mine-laying vessels in the Strait of Hormuz after intelligence indicated Iran was preparing to deploy naval mines in the world's busiest oil shipping channel; the U.S. Energy Secretary briefly posted and then deleted a claim that the Navy had escorted a tanker through the strait.
  • Iran's ambassador to Cyprus confirmed that new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei sustained leg injuries in the strike that killed his father, former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, and six other family members; Mojtaba Khamenei has not appeared publicly since being announced as successor three days ago.
  • Israel launched what it described as "large-scale" strikes on southern Beirut suburbs and central Beirut's waterfront, killing at least seven people in one strike near tents sheltering displaced families; Hezbollah fired dozens of rockets into Israel in a joint operation with Iran targeting more than 50 military sites.
  • The UN Security Council adopted a resolution, co-sponsored by 135 countries, condemning Iran's attacks on Gulf states; separately, Spain formally dismissed its ambassador to Israel, and the EU and UK demanded Israel halt a surge in West Bank settler violence that has killed six Palestinians since the war began.
Middle East — Forward Look

What to Expect Next

  • Analysts expect Iran may attempt to deploy sea mines or further unmanned vessel attacks in the Strait of Hormuz; historically, Iran's 1988 mining campaign during the "Tanker War" severely disrupted Gulf shipping and triggered direct U.S. naval engagements, suggesting a potential for further military escalation at sea.
  • Observers are monitoring whether Yemen-based Houthi forces will reopen attacks on Red Sea shipping as U.S. warships approach the Bab el-Mandeb chokepoint; a resumption would compound the existing Hormuz disruptions and could further strain global energy and trade routes.
  • The confirmed school strike in Minab and rising civilian casualties historically intensify domestic and international political pressure on the U.S.; analysts expect congressional scrutiny to increase and allied governments may face growing calls to distance themselves from the campaign.
  • With Iran's new supreme leader wounded and out of public view, and Iranian authorities warning protesters will be treated as enemies, analysts expect a period of internal uncertainty in Tehran that could affect both the coherence of Iran's military response and any potential diplomatic off-ramp.
  • Global economic disruption — including oil above $100, airline rerouting, financial institution evacuations in Gulf cities, and heating oil price surges — is expected to intensify if Hormuz shipping remains contested; historically, sustained oil price shocks of this magnitude have preceded broader economic slowdowns.
