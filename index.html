Today in the News

  • Israeli forces shot and killed four members of a Palestinian family — a mother, father, and two boys aged five and seven — after opening fire on their car in the northern occupied West Bank as they returned from a Ramadan shopping trip; two other children in the vehicle survived, according to the Palestinian Health Ministry and multiple international outlets.

  • The Strait of Hormuz recorded its first full day with zero ship transits as Iran maintained its blockade; Brent crude traded above $105 per barrel, and Trump called on NATO allies and China to help assemble a coalition to reopen the waterway, warning of consequences for countries that refused — a demand met with tepid international responses.

Middle East — Forward Look

What to Expect Next

  • Historically, full closures of the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of global oil passes — have triggered rapid international diplomatic mobilisation; analysts expect intensified negotiations among Gulf states, European nations, and China over convoy arrangements or a maritime coalition in coming days, though political costs of aligning with the US campaign may delay commitments.
  • Israel's announcement of "limited ground operations" in southern Lebanon mirrors language used in its 2024 incursion and in the 2006 war; historically, such operations have tended to expand in scope, and analysts expect further escalation along the Israel-Lebanon border, particularly if Hezbollah rocket fire on northern Israeli cities like Kiryat Shmona continues.
  • With oil above $105 per barrel and UK lenders already pulling mortgage products, economists warn that prolonged disruption to energy routes could produce broader economic fallout; historically, sustained oil price shocks of this magnitude have preceded recessions in import-dependent economies, and analysts expect further fiscal interventions from European governments.
  • The killing of the Palestinian family in the West Bank is likely to draw further international scrutiny to Israeli use of force in the territory; historically, such incidents during periods of broader regional conflict have intensified diplomatic pressure at the UN and strained bilateral relationships with key allies.
  • Trump's public statements rejecting a ceasefire while simultaneously demanding allied military support suggest the conflict's timeline remains open-ended; analysts expect growing domestic and international debate over war authorisation, particularly as US military casualties mount and Congressional figures from both parties begin to voice concerns.
