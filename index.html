Global Overview

Today in the News

  • The US military acknowledged, via a preliminary inquiry, that a US Tomahawk cruise missile was likely responsible for a strike on an Iranian girls' school that killed at least 165–175 people, most of them children, in what would rank among the highest civilian casualty events of the conflict now in its 13th day.
  • Iran escalated attacks on energy infrastructure across the Gulf, setting ablaze two tankers in Iraqi waters, striking a fuel facility in Bahrain, and hitting an oil storage site at Oman's Port of Salalah, as global oil prices surged past $100 a barrel despite US and allied releases of emergency petroleum reserves.
  • The US military said it destroyed 16 Iranian mine-laying vessels near the Strait of Hormuz after intelligence indicated Iran was preparing to deploy naval mines in the waterway through which approximately 20% of the world's oil supply transits; three merchant ships were also struck by projectiles in the strait.
  • Israel launched what it described as "large-scale" strikes on Hezbollah targets in Beirut's southern suburbs and southern Lebanon, following a joint Hezbollah-Iran attack on more than 50 targets including Israeli military bases; an Israeli "double-tap strike" on Beirut's seafront killed eight displaced people.
  • Iran's sports minister announced the country would not participate in the 2026 FIFA World Cup, while Australia granted asylum to seven members of Iran's national women's soccer team, one of whom subsequently reversed her decision and asked to return.
  • Australia relaxed fuel quality standards for 60 days to allow importation of 100 million additional litres of petrol, while Southeast Asian nations including Indonesia and Vietnam began limiting travel and closing offices due to the deepening oil supply crisis.
Forward Look

What to Expect Next

  • Analysts expect oil prices to remain elevated or climb further if Iran follows through on deploying naval mines in the Strait of Hormuz; historically, mine-laying operations in the Gulf during the 1980s Iran-Iraq "Tanker War" caused prolonged shipping disruptions and required months-long international minesweeping efforts.
  • The confirmed US responsibility for the Iranian school strike is likely to intensify international pressure on Washington, as historically comparable incidents — such as the 1991 Amiriyah shelter bombing in Iraq — have become turning points in public opinion and diplomatic negotiations during US military campaigns.
  • Iran's campaign targeting Gulf state energy infrastructure may pressure countries like Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, and Oman to seek de-escalation with Tehran; historically, Gulf states have acted to limit their exposure when directly targeted, as seen during the 2019 Aramco attacks.
  • Hezbollah's renewed engagement from Lebanon, combined with Iran's direct strikes on Israel, raises the prospect of a sustained multi-front conflict; observers are watching whether Yemen-based Houthi forces reopen hostilities near the Red Sea's Bab el-Mandeb strait, which would further disrupt global shipping.
  • The global economic ripple effects — rising fuel costs, airline surcharges, food price pressures, and stock market losses — are expected to intensify in coming days, with analysts warning that prolonged disruption to Gulf energy flows could trigger broader recessionary pressures, particularly in fuel-import-dependent economies across Southeast Asia and Africa.

Middle East

Today in the News

  • The United States military struck and destroyed 16 Iranian mine-laying vessels near the Strait of Hormuz after US intelligence indicated Iran was preparing to deploy naval mines in the waterway, through which approximately 20% of global oil supply transits.
  • Iran escalated attacks on energy infrastructure across the Gulf region, setting ablaze two tankers in Iraqi waters, striking a fuel facility in Bahrain's Muharraq, and launching drones toward Saudi oil fields — both countries confirmed intercepting the majority of incoming projectiles.
  • Global oil prices surged past $100 per barrel despite coordinated emergency reserve releases by the US and allied nations, as Iranian attacks on commercial shipping and Gulf infrastructure intensified fears of a prolonged energy supply disruption.
  • A preliminary US military inquiry indicated that a US Tomahawk cruise missile was responsible for a strike on an Iranian girls' school that killed at least 165–175 people, most of them children, in what officials described as a targeting error — prompting calls from a Republican senator for the US to publicly acknowledge responsibility if confirmed.
  • Israel launched what it described as "large-scale" strikes on Hezbollah targets in Beirut's southern suburbs and southern Lebanon, following a joint Iranian-Hezbollah attack on more than 50 targets including Israeli military bases; a separate Israeli strike on Beirut's seafront killed eight displaced people in what observers described as an attempted assassination.
  • Iran's sports minister announced the country would withdraw from the 2026 FIFA World Cup, scheduled to be held in the United States, while seven members of Iran's women's national soccer team were granted asylum by Australia during a tournament there.
Middle East — Forward Look

What to Expect Next

  • Analysts expect the Strait of Hormuz to remain a critical flashpoint; historically, Iran's 1980s "Tanker War" mining campaign during the Iran-Iraq war led to US naval Operation Earnest Will, and similar escalation could prompt expanded US naval convoy operations and further maritime confrontations.
  • Oil market analysts warn that sustained prices above $100 per barrel, combined with disrupted Hormuz transit, could trigger broader global economic consequences including food price inflation, as rising energy and fertilizer costs historically cascade through agricultural supply chains.
  • Iran's proxy network — particularly Hezbollah in Lebanon and the Houthis in Yemen — may calibrate their level of engagement in coming days; observers are watching whether Houthis resume attacks on Red Sea shipping as US warships approach, which would open a second major chokepoint to global trade.
  • The confirmed US responsibility for the school strike, if formally acknowledged, historically tends to become a significant inflection point in public opinion both domestically and internationally, as occurred with incidents in Iraq and Afghanistan, potentially increasing diplomatic pressure for a ceasefire framework.
  • Diplomatic efforts may intensify as the EU, France, and Turkey have signaled growing unease with the conflict's expansion; historically, wars involving energy supply disruption of this scale have accelerated back-channel negotiations once economic costs mount for all parties involved.
